# Where Did All the Soul Go?

The nostalgia, whether it's real or not, brings us to the past where we tend to idealize things. How does it relate to software then? Well, we could probably start with video games, like GTA series or Mafia, going through NFS Underground, Max Payne, Half Life, or even rally sims - Richard Burns Rally. Remember Clippy? It was annoying, a bit clunky, and basic, but it had a soul and some "human-touch". Even apps like WinAmp, or RainMeter had whole communities behind them. People were spending weekends building their perfect desktop setups and sharing them proudly. ICQ gave you a number as an identity and a sound - the iconic "uh-oh" - that's burned into an entire generation's memory. Shazam felt like witchcraft, you'd hold your phone to a speaker and it would name the song. People showed strangers this at parties. Word Lens rewrote foreign text through your camera in real time, translating it visually before your eyes. Google bought it in 2014 and it quietly disappeared.

Web development? Maybe it wasn't so mature as it is now, no TypeScript, no frameworks like Next or component libraries like ShadCN. However, for some reason I feel like even the web felt more individual and had more of a personal touch. I recall watching Kevin Powell's CSS Challenges, or meetups where people were showing their art built with CSS over weeks. Today, we have more tools than ever, the quality definitely went up, yet for some reason the standardization killed the individuality. Especially these days where so many web pages are generated by AI with premade components. It looks decent, but it's also boring and very often I have a feeling that I have seen the same design 100x times before.

The same applies to other things as well, just look at the cars, how they used to look and how similar each brand is to the other now. Movies? Just look at the top list of IMDb - The Matrix, Fight Club, Pulp Fiction and the list goes on. Most of the films are over 20 years old. Music? Is there anyone who remembers MTV Unplugged concerts? Hair Metal vs Grunge. Bands like Black Sabbath, The Rolling Stones, Alice In Chains, Pearl Jam. Pop with Michael Jackson, David Bowie, or No Doubt. Hip-Hop culture represented by Eminem, Run DMC, and 2Pac. Don't you feel like everything had much more of its own taste to it? Maybe people were way less afraid of being cringe or judged for being themselves and didn't stay as neutral as today just to avoid it. Maybe it was the symbol of the 90s, and new possibilities as well as exploration with all of the mistakes on the way. Maybe it's scarcity that boosts creativity. Or maybe we are now simply more mature and we make way less mistakes? Yet, once again - where is all the soul and fun?

## The Challenge

AI can now hear, see, and even speak - the problem here is that AI is also by its nature not deterministic, and it's rather a probability. When it comes to fitness and exercising, where every subtle move matters and the difference can happen in milliseconds, plus on top of that everyone has different body proportions and style, even these small deviations can make the whole thing collapse. That's why building a proper real-time personal fitness coach app is still a challenge until this day. Keep in mind that I am not a professional athlete, nor do I have any professional background, so please take all of these things with a bit of distance and a healthy common sense. Probably, to achieve very high accuracy, we would need sensors all over the body and a very fast model that would learn and adjust to each given person. Technically it sounds feasible, but I haven't seen any app doing proper fitness AI coaching yet. Technically, we could just spin up Gemini Live, give it a proper prompt and let it guide us. The problem is that it's not accurate at all, and as far as I know it takes snapshots at up to 1fps. Currently, after spending years on research and prototyping - I can say that body pose recognition ML models seem to be the most reliable, yet still far from perfect solution.

When we look into the projects, most of the time I can see squats as the default demo exercise, and it's most likely not because squats are relatively easy and most people can do them anywhere without any equipment - it's rather about technical limitations. A very basic squats recognition app would track the position of the user's knees and hips. The models are light enough to run them several times a second on random frames from the camera feed. What we get behind the scenes are most of the time x and y coordinates of each body part. As you may probably imagine now, it's now only about checking the distance between the hips and knees, or the angle between hips, knees, and ankles. To understand it even better we can have a look at the documentation from Apple here: https://developer.apple.com/documentation/vision/detecting-human-body-poses-in-images

![Human Body Pose](/images/bodypoints.png)
nts-

I am using the Apple example as I found their model working possibly the best with very fair performance, yet there are many other similar models on the market. My point here was to just make a reliable and stable app that would run without hiccups on a mobile device.

To sum up, from a very high level, these apps work in the following order:
1. Camera feed streams the frames
2. Some of the frames each second are processed by a tiny ML model running on the device (most of the time we would skip some of the frames to avoid memory leaks and improve performance)
3. ML model returns x and y coordinates for each frame
4. Each such loop calls a function that saves the positions of each body part, sometimes also taking the average of the last few frames to compensate the error margin
5. Another method takes in the saved coordinates and the schema, checking for each phase of the exercise
6. The results are rendered on the screen

The mechanism is relatively simple, and it doesn't even require having any sophisticated AI systems in place. The problem here is that we still do have limited data and not many reference points if we want the app to be universal for various users, as most of us are different - some of us have longer legs, others a longer torso and so on. That's why so many real-time fitness apps work great with squats, as the logic is quite simple. Things get tricky very fast when the light is changing, the angle, or the exercise. Push-ups would be another simple example that is not so simple anymore, as it's difficult to track the user's position checking the angle of the arms and the position of the hips and knees at the same time from the same camera. With all of these we are quickly running into the trap of hardcoding some values that should work best for most users, that actually will most likely not work that well. These kind of apps also require a lot of presumptions, like that in a pull-ups app the user really does have a pull up bar and that they are not cheating. The list goes on, not to mention that with such an app safety should also be a priority - how can you be sure that the user won't get injured during the workout. That's why it also requires the user's trust in the app, complex Terms of Service and so on. Nevertheless, I still do think that having such an app would be pretty useful, and that sooner or later we will find a way to break the barriers.

## FormKit Boilerplate Code

Arnold Schwarzenegger says "Don't Complain. Do Something." This is my movement then - let's do something together, at least in the fitness apps scene. FormKit was something that I had on my mind for over 2 years - a boilerplate code for iOS with a ready camera setup and skeleton, to work on additional exercises. Since I worked on camera vision based apps for a while, I already had the code ready, just reusing it each time I created a new app. The boilerplate code on the Apple site only mentions how to use it with a single static image instead of a continuous camera feed, not to mention the exercise code logic.

If you're reading this and would also like to either contribute to the boilerplate code of FormKit, or maybe create a new app based on it, check out the repo here: https://github.com/LachPawel/FormKit

With FormKit you get a ready setup out of the box, with the camera preview, Apple Vision, and skeleton ready. Note that the code is not compatible with macOS. The architecture is very similar but it will most likely not work on macOS - hence it's a nice low hanging fruit for a small open source contribution. Another thing is that you need a physical iOS device to run the code, as the simulator will most likely not support the camera preview. It is also the code I have been testing for over 2 years, and the performance should be quite stable with around 30-60mb of RAM used with the camera and ML on, as well as around 30% CPU usage.

Once you spin up the code you should see the camera preview, and when there is a person in the frame, the skeleton with debug values should appear on the screen.

![FormKit](/images/formkit.png)

 The main part is happening inside of the `PoseEstimator.swift` file where we take into account only every 3rd frame for performance reasons, then each 3rd frame is passed to the method called `analyzeFrame` where the core calculation of the body pose happens via `DetectHumanBodyPoseRequest()` - this is the exact place where the Apple Vision ML model infers the possible body pose coordinates from the given frame. The rest is just a wrapper on top of the values we get out of there :)

```swift
// Downsample — process every 3rd frame only
guard _frameCounter % 3 == 0, !_isAnalyzingFrame else {
    CMSampleBufferInvalidate(sampleBuffer)
    return
}

_isAnalyzingFrame = true

Task { [weak self] in
    await self?.analyzeFrame(frame: sampleBuffer)
}
```

That's all in essence about the base FormKit boilerplate. Feel free to take the code from here to anywhere you want - and the app doesn't even have to be a fitness app, I can imagine using it for a game controller or something similar.

## The Handstand

All right, so with this we can take the boilerplate and create the first app on top of it. My aim was to create something more than just a squats counter, but something that I would really need since I am not always assisted by someone who could check on the correctness of my handstand training. Be aware though - use this code at your own risk! I'm not a professional, and it's just a simple prototype app that may give completely wrong results, so take it with a huge grain of salt.

The code can be found here on the handstand branch of the FormKit repo: https://github.com/LachPawel/FormKit/tree/feature/wall-handstand

Having the boilerplate with camera preview and skeleton ready we can finally add some custom logic - in my case it was about checking if I am in a straight pose during the handstand or am I bent. At this point it's also worth mentioning that there are many more variables, so we are only scratching the surface here. To truly analyze the handstand we would probably need different angles and even a better body pose model with more data, such as the angle of the wrists and so on. One of the tips my calisthenics trainer gave me was to twist my pelvis just like Michael Jackson did in his famous dances - and that sparked another idea for a small easter egg in the app that would remind me about this by playing a 2 second video of Michael Jackson doing the pelvis twist :D

[![Michael Jackson Handstand](https://img.youtube.com/vi/juyOQs-ugmM/maxresdefault.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=juyOQs-ugmM)

The essence of the implementation can be found in the file called `HandstandAnalyzer.swift`. The code is quite self-explanatory, and as you can see it's mainly about handling the values from the Apple Vision API, with some basic math behind it. This is how most of these kinds of apps work nowadays. You just run the ML model over several frames, extract the values out of it and process the data accordingly. In this way we also lower the lack of determinism to the minimum - we operate on concrete data extracted from the images, and we can also run it much faster, unlike with traditional LLMs. I have seen already some super fast models like FastVLM from Apple that could possibly work in the future, but when I tested it last year it was far from any acceptable level. My bet would be that in a couple of years we may see such models trained for these cases, and then it would potentially open the door to a new generation of live apps. Still, at the moment of writing this, I would risk saying that the approach mentioned above is the best from all of the available options.

```swift
// HandstandAnalyzer.swift
// Analyses whether the body is straight in a SIDE-VIEW wall handstand.
//
// ── Setup ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
//   • Phone is placed to the LEFT or RIGHT of the user, in portrait orientation.
//   • The user faces the wall, inverted, with head at the bottom and feet at top.
//
// ── Vision coordinate system (normalised, origin bottom-left) ────────────────
//   Feet / ankles  →  HIGH y  (top of frame,  y ≈ 1.0)
//   Hips           →  mid-high y
//   Shoulders      →  mid-low  y
//   Head / nose    →  LOW y   (near floor,    y ≈ 0.1–0.2)
//   Wrists / hands →  LOWEST y (on the floor, y < nose.y)
//
// ── Checks ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
//   1. Body vertical      – every segment deviates < toleranceDeg from vertical
//   2. Head below hips    – nose.y  <  hipMid.y   (inverted position confirmed)
//   3. Arms straight      – elbow angle ≈ 180 °   (locked-out arms)
//   4. Wrists below head  – wristMid.y < nose.y   (hands on floor, below head)
//
// ── Side-view note ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
//   From a profile angle Vision may detect both left & right joints stacked
//   on top of each other, or only the near side.  We use bilateral midpoints
//   where both sides fire, and fall back to whichever single side is visible.

import Foundation
import Vision
import CoreGraphics

// MARK: - Public types

/// Three-level quality rating for a single segment or the whole pose.
enum AlignmentQuality {
    case good    // green  — within tight tolerance
    case close   // yellow — within loose tolerance, nearly there
    case bad     // red    — clearly out of alignment
}

/// Alignment result for a single check.
struct SegmentAlignment {
    let name: String
    /// Deviation from the ideal in degrees (0 = perfect).
    let deviationDeg: Double
    /// Three-level quality for this segment.
    let quality: AlignmentQuality
    /// Convenience: true when quality == .good
    var isAligned: Bool { quality == .good }
}

/// Full alignment snapshot for one pose frame.
struct HandstandAlignment {
    let segments: [SegmentAlignment]
    /// True only when every segment is .good.
    let isFullyAligned: Bool
    /// True when every segment is .good or .close (no .bad).
    let isNearlyAligned: Bool
    /// Human-readable description of what is wrong (empty when aligned).
    let feedbackMessage: String
    /// Overall lateral deviation of the whole body from vertical.
    let overallDeviationDeg: Double
}

// MARK: - Analyzer

struct HandstandAnalyzer {
    /// Tight tolerance — segments within this are GREEN (good form).
    var goodTolerance: Double  = 28.0
    /// Loose tolerance — segments within this are YELLOW (close enough, minor tweak needed).
    var closeTolerance: Double = 50.0
    /// Max degrees the elbow may deviate from 180° for a green rating.
    var elbowGoodTolerance: Double  = 32.0
    var elbowCloseTolerance: Double = 52.0
    /// Minimum Vision confidence for a joint to be used.
    var minConfidence: Float = 0.30

    func analyse(
        joints: [HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName: Joint]
    ) -> HandstandAlignment {

        // ── helpers ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
        func pt(_ name: HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName) -> CGPoint? {
            guard let j = joints[name], j.confidence >= minConfidence else { return nil }
            return CGPoint(x: j.location.x, y: j.location.y)
        }

        func mid(_ a: CGPoint, _ b: CGPoint) -> CGPoint {
            CGPoint(x: (a.x + b.x) / 2, y: (a.y + b.y) / 2)
        }

        func bilateral(
            _ left: HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName,
            _ right: HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName
        ) -> CGPoint? {
            switch (pt(left), pt(right)) {
            case let (l?, r?): return mid(l, r)
            case let (l?, nil): return l
            case let (nil, r?): return r
            default: return nil
            }
        }

        func angleFromVertical(_ a: CGPoint, _ b: CGPoint) -> Double {
            let dx = b.x - a.x, dy = b.y - a.y
            guard abs(dx) > 1e-6 || abs(dy) > 1e-6 else { return 0 }
            return atan2(abs(dx), abs(dy)) * 180 / .pi
        }

        func angleAtJoint(a: CGPoint, vertex: CGPoint, b: CGPoint) -> Double {
            let v1 = CGPoint(x: a.x - vertex.x, y: a.y - vertex.y)
            let v2 = CGPoint(x: b.x - vertex.x, y: b.y - vertex.y)
            let dot = v1.x * v2.x + v1.y * v2.y
            let mag1 = sqrt(v1.x * v1.x + v1.y * v1.y)
            let mag2 = sqrt(v2.x * v2.x + v2.y * v2.y)
            guard mag1 > 1e-6, mag2 > 1e-6 else { return 180 }
            return acos(max(-1, min(1, dot / (mag1 * mag2)))) * 180 / .pi
        }

        func verticalQuality(_ dev: Double) -> AlignmentQuality {
            if dev <= goodTolerance  { return .good  }
            if dev <= closeTolerance { return .close }
            return .bad
        }

        // ── derive centre-line points ────────────────────────────────────────
        let wristPt    = bilateral(.leftWrist,    .rightWrist)
        let elbowPt    = bilateral(.leftElbow,    .rightElbow)
        let shoulderPt = bilateral(.leftShoulder, .rightShoulder)
        let hipPt      = bilateral(.leftHip,      .rightHip)
        let kneePt     = bilateral(.leftKnee,     .rightKnee)
        let anklePt    = bilateral(.leftAnkle,    .rightAnkle)
        let nosePt     = pt(.nose)

        // ── HARD GATE: body must be inverted ─────────────────────────────────
        // In Vision coords y=0 is bottom, y=1 is top of frame.
        // When upright:   ankles.y < hips.y  (ankles lower in frame)
        // When inverted:  ankles.y > hips.y  (feet above hips, near top of frame)
        // If this gate fails we return immediately — no segment checks run.
        guard let anklePtGate = anklePt, let hipPtGate = hipPt,
              anklePtGate.y > hipPtGate.y + 0.05 else {
            return HandstandAlignment(
                segments: [],
                isFullyAligned: false,
                isNearlyAligned: false,
                feedbackMessage: "Get inverted first – feet must be above hips",
                overallDeviationDeg: 0
            )
        }

        var segments: [SegmentAlignment] = []
        var messages: [String] = []

        // ── 1. Vertical alignment ────────────────────────────────────────────
        func addVertical(name: String, from a: CGPoint?, to b: CGPoint?) {
            guard let a, let b else { return }
            let dev = angleFromVertical(a, b)
            let q   = verticalQuality(dev)
            segments.append(SegmentAlignment(name: name, deviationDeg: dev, quality: q))
            if q == .bad   { messages.append("\(name) tilted \(Int(dev))°") }
        }

        addVertical(name: "Arms",   from: wristPt,    to: shoulderPt)
        addVertical(name: "Torso",  from: shoulderPt, to: hipPt)
        addVertical(name: "Thighs", from: hipPt,      to: kneePt)
        addVertical(name: "Shins",  from: kneePt,     to: anklePt)

        // ── 2. Head below hips ───────────────────────────────────────────────
        if let nose = nosePt, let hip = hipPt {
            let headBelowHips = nose.y < hip.y
            let dev = headBelowHips ? 0.0 : abs(nose.y - hip.y) * 100
            let q: AlignmentQuality = headBelowHips ? .good : .bad
            segments.append(SegmentAlignment(name: "HeadDown", deviationDeg: dev, quality: q))
            if !headBelowHips { messages.append("Head not below hips – are you inverted?") }
        }

        // ── 3. Arms straight (elbow angle ≈ 180°) ────────────────────────────
        func elbowQuality(
            wristName: HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName,
            elbowName: HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName,
            shoulderName: HumanBodyPoseObservation.PoseJointName
        ) -> (AlignmentQuality, Double)? {
            guard let w = pt(wristName), let e = pt(elbowName), let s = pt(shoulderName) else { return nil }
            let bend = 180 - angleAtJoint(a: w, vertex: e, b: s)
            let q: AlignmentQuality
            if bend <= elbowGoodTolerance  { q = .good  }
            else if bend <= elbowCloseTolerance { q = .close }
            else { q = .bad }
            return (q, bend)
        }

        let leftElbowResult  = elbowQuality(wristName: .leftWrist,  elbowName: .leftElbow,  shoulderName: .leftShoulder)
        let rightElbowResult = elbowQuality(wristName: .rightWrist, elbowName: .rightElbow, shoulderName: .rightShoulder)

        if let (q, dev) = leftElbowResult ?? rightElbowResult {
            segments.append(SegmentAlignment(name: "ArmsStraight", deviationDeg: dev, quality: q))
            if q == .bad { messages.append("Bend in elbows – straighten your arms") }
        }

        // ── 4. Wrists below head ─────────────────────────────────────────────
        if let wrist = wristPt, let nose = nosePt {
            let ok = wrist.y < nose.y
            let dev = ok ? 0.0 : abs(wrist.y - nose.y) * 100
            let q: AlignmentQuality = ok ? .good : .bad
            segments.append(SegmentAlignment(name: "WristsBelowHead", deviationDeg: dev, quality: q))
            if !ok { messages.append("Hands should be below your head") }
        }

        // ── Overall banana check ─────────────────────────────────────────────
        var overallDev: Double = 0
        if let top = wristPt ?? shoulderPt, let bottom = anklePt ?? kneePt {
            overallDev = angleFromVertical(top, bottom)
            if overallDev > closeTolerance + 4, messages.isEmpty {
                messages.append("Body curved – engage your core")
            }
        }

        // ── Decision ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
        guard !segments.isEmpty else {
            return HandstandAlignment(
                segments: [],
                isFullyAligned: false,
                isNearlyAligned: false,
                feedbackMessage: "Cannot see your body – position the camera to the side",
                overallDeviationDeg: 0
            )
        }

        let worstQuality = segments.map(\.quality).max { a, b in
            // bad > close > good
            let rank: (AlignmentQuality) -> Int = { $0 == .bad ? 2 : $0 == .close ? 1 : 0 }
            return rank(a) < rank(b)
        } ?? .good

        let isFullyAligned  = worstQuality == .good  && overallDev <= goodTolerance + 4
        let isNearlyAligned = worstQuality != .bad   && overallDev <= closeTolerance + 4

        return HandstandAlignment(
            segments: segments,
            isFullyAligned: isFullyAligned,
            isNearlyAligned: isNearlyAligned,
            feedbackMessage: messages.joined(separator: " · "),
            overallDeviationDeg: overallDev
        )
    }
}
```
